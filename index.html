Introduction

The Hanunuo Mangyan are one of the eight indigenous Mangyan groups living in the island of Mindoro. Known for their rich cultural heritage, they are recognized for preserving traditional practices, language, and writing systems despite the influence of modernization. The Hanunuo Mangyans mainly reside in the southern part of Mindoro, where they depend on agriculture, weaving, and forest resources for their livelihood. Their way of life reflects a deep connection with nature, emphasizing simplicity, cooperation, and respect for the environment.

One of the most remarkable aspects of the Hanunuo Mangyan culture is their ancient script called Surat Mangyan, a traditional writing system used for poetry, songs, and communication. They are also known for their practices in agroforestry, particularly kaingin or swidden farming, which has long been part of their sustainable living. Through their customs, beliefs, and indigenous knowledge, the Hanunuo Mangyans continue to contribute to the cultural diversity and history of the Philippines.

History

A luminous timeline of origin, migration, resistance, and the evolving demography that defines Indigenous history.

01

Origin

Mangyan people The name “Mangyan” refers to a certain group or groups of ethnic Filipinos, who are predominantly inhabiting the interior areas of the Island of Mindoro.Various reports, however, indicate them to have been present in Tablas, Romblon and Sibuyan Islands, east of Mindoro, as well as in Palawan, Negros and Albay. Among the Mangyans of Mindoro themselves, the name Mangyan, like among the Alangan or Iraya, simply means: man, woman, person, or human being, without reference to any tribe or nation. As to Mangyan as a tribal name, this is claimed specifically by the ethnic group in Mansalay and Bulalacao municipalities. And if they want to stress the point, they might say: “Kami hanunuo Mangyan,” or in Tagalog: “Kami’y tutuong Mangyan,” because “hanunuo,” from the root word “tuo,” is an adjective meaning: true, real. To call these Mangyan just “Hanunuo,” would not make sense to them without adding a substantive.

02

Migrations

Inland Retreat: Originally coastal dwellers, the Hanunuo migrated inland to the mountains to avoid the influx of Spanish settlers, religious conversion, and Moro slave raids. Modern Pressures: More recently, they have faced displacement from ancestral domains due to land grabbing, deforestation, and private pasture leases. Labor Migration: In recent decades, younger Hanunuo members have increasingly migrated out of their communities to lowlands for work, a shift that threatens close-knit kinship ties and traditional subsistence farming.

03

Colonization & Resistance

The Hanunuo are one of the Indigenous Mangyan groups of Mindoro in the Philippines. Before colonization, they lived in coastal and lowland areas where they practiced farming, trade, hunting, and maintained their own culture and writing system called Surat Mangyan. During the Spanish colonization in the 16th century, many Mangyan groups, including the Hanunuo, resisted foreign control, forced Christianization, and settlement expansion. Instead of fully submitting to colonial rule, they moved deeper into the mountains and forest interiors of Mindoro to protect their traditions, land, and identity.

04

Demography

Originally coastal dwellers, the Hanunuo migrated inland to the mountains to avoid the influx of Spanish settlers, religious conversion, and Moro slave raids. More recently, they have faced displacement from ancestral domains due to land grabbing, deforestation, and private pasture leases. In recent decades, younger Hanunuo members have increasingly migrated out of their communities to lowlands for work, a shift that threatens close-knit kinship ties and traditional subsistence farming.

Reflection

The history of the Hanunuo Mangyan reflects a long journey of resilience, identity, and adaptation shaped by both their environment and outside influences. From their early existence in coastal and lowland areas, they gradually migrated into the mountainous interior of Mindoro as a way to protect their culture, traditions, and way of life from colonization, religious conversion, and external control. This movement was not just physical relocation but also a cultural effort to preserve their language, beliefs, and their ancient writing system despite constant pressures from outside forces. Over time, their history became closely tied to resistance and survival. During the Spanish colonization, the Hanunuo chose to retreat further into the forests rather than fully submit to foreign rule, allowing them to safeguard their customs and identity. Even in more recent times, they continue to face challenges such as land grabbing, deforestation, and loss of ancestral domains, which affect their traditional way of life and connection to the land. Today, their demographic situation is also changing, as younger members increasingly migrate to lowland areas in search of work, which weakens traditional kinship ties and subsistence farming practices. Despite these struggles, the Hanunuo Mangyan remain resilient, continuing to preserve their cultural heritage while adapting to modern pressures, showing that their history is not only one of survival but also of enduring strength and continuity.

Social

Social structure, identity, and environmental guardianship are central to Indigenous life and modern challenges.

🌱

Relationship to Land

The relationship between the Hanunóo Mangyan people and their land is profound, spiritually rooted, and holistic. For the Hanunóo, land is life. It goes far beyond a physical location; it is the cornerstone of their cultural identity, sustenance, and generational survival. The Hanunóo, based primarily in the mountains of southern Mindoro, rely on a highly specialized, sustainable form of swidden agriculture (locally called kaingin). Rather than destroying the forest, they skillfully rotate crops and practice fallow periods, ensuring the land remains fertile.

🌀

Social Identity

The social identity of the Hanunuo Mangyan is strongly connected to their identity as an indigenous community that values tradition, kinship, culture, and harmony with nature. Based on the reference, they identify themselves as “Mangyan” and distinguish themselves from outsiders or non-Mangyan people, whom they call “damuong.” Their identity is preserved through their traditional practices, language, clothing, customs, and way of life despite outside influences.

🌿

Environmental Preservation

The Hanunuo Mangyans practice three main agroforestry systems: swidden or kaingin farming, multistorey farming, and home gardens. Among these, kaingin farming is the most commonly practiced and plays an important role in supporting their livelihood and environment. Traditional farming practices and sustainability were examined through soil analyses, interviews, and other methods. Kaingin farms are commonly planted with rice, corn, banana, cassava, ube, and kadios, creating a multiple cropping system that provides different food sources. Farmers also allow the land to rest for one to three years before planting again. These practices help the Hanunuo Mangyans maintain their food supply while preserving their traditional agricultural knowledge.

⚖️

Social Structure

Their social identity is also shaped by their close family and community relationships. The Hanunuo Mangyan are known for having an egalitarian and leaderless society where elders are respected because of their wisdom rather than authority. Cooperation, peace, and communal unity are important values in their community. Their language and the traditional Surat Mangyan writing system are considered symbols of pride and identity because these help preserve their culture and traditions across generations.

🛡️

Modern Issues

As generations continue to change, the Hanunuo Mangyan community has also experienced the effects of modernization in many parts of daily life. Hanunuo millennials today are more exposed to technology, social media, and modern lifestyles compared to the older generation. Changes can be seen in the way they communicate, their views on marriage, and even their political beliefs. While these modern influences continue to shape the younger generation, the values and traditions taught by their elders still remain an important part of their identity. Even with the fast-changing world, many Hanunuo millennials continue to protect and preserve their culture. Younger members of the community are still being taught their traditions, language, and customs through local learning centers and community guidance. Modern technology may have introduced a new way of living, but it has not completely erased their cultural roots. Instead, many Hanunuo people continue to balance tradition and modernization, showing that cultural identity can still survive and grow in today’s modern society.

Social Reflection

The social life of the Hanunuo Mangyan shows a deep and meaningful connection between people, land, and identity. Their way of life is strongly rooted in the belief that land is not just a resource but the foundation of their survival, culture, and spiritual well-being. Through their traditional swidden farming practices, they show how knowledge of the environment is passed down through generations while still maintaining balance with nature. This relationship highlights how their identity is closely shaped by their environment and how their everyday life reflects respect, cooperation, and sustainability. At the same time, their social structure reflects equality and strong community bonds, where decisions are often guided by elders and shared understanding rather than strict authority. Their culture also shows resilience in the face of modern challenges, as younger generations experience changes brought by technology, education, and outside influences. Despite this, many still continue to value and preserve their traditions, language, and customs, showing a strong effort to balance modern life with cultural heritage. This reflects a community that is adapting to change while still holding on to its roots and identity.

Political

Political systems blend customary law, administration, and modern governance to honor sovereignty and cultural rights.

Political Systems

The Hanunoo Mangyan community is known for having a peaceful and equal society. They do not have chiefs, servants, or formally recognized rulers. Instead, members of the community treat one another with respect and equality. One important figure in their society is the panudlakan, a spiritual leader who performs special tasks and guides the community in cultural and religious practices. Peace and order are maintained through understanding and cooperation among community members. The Hanunoo Mangyan people are generally peaceful, and conflicts are usually settled through discussions led by elders. Their goal is to achieve reconciliation between both sides instead of punishment. Small fines may also be given, which are accepted and understood within their traditional customs.

Administration

The Hanunuo Mangyan have a simple and community-based political administration rooted in their traditional customs and kinship system. Their society does not follow a strict hierarchical form of government like modern political systems. Instead, leadership is usually informal, with respected elders and community leaders guiding decision-making, settling conflicts, and maintaining peace within the group. These elders are valued for their wisdom, experience, and knowledge of traditions. Decisions in the community are often made through discussion and mutual agreement rather than through formal laws or authority.

Rules & Regulations

The Hanunuo Mangyan people of southern Mindoro follow unwritten customs and traditions instead of written laws. Their society is guided by cooperation, respect, and moral values passed down by elders through generations. Community practices help regulate justice, marriage, sharing, and the peaceful settlement of conflicts. One important traditional practice is the kasaba, a form of trial by ordeal used for serious accusations such as theft or adultery, where a suspect may place their hand in boiling water to prove innocence or guilt. If found guilty, the person may be asked to pay fines or prepare a communal feast as a way of restoring peace and harmony within the community.

Customary Law

The Hanunuo Mangyans follow customary laws based on tradition, respect, and community values. These laws are not written formally but are passed down through generations and guide behavior, conflict resolution, marriage, land use, and relationships within the community. Elders play an important role in interpreting these customs and helping settle disputes through discussion, advice, and mutual agreement rather than punishment. Their customary laws focus more on restoring harmony and maintaining peaceful relationships among families and clans. In the modern era, a political system is evident among the Hanunuo Mangyans through the influence of the Philippine government and local governance structures. Many communities now interact with barangay officials, local government units, and Indigenous Peoples organizations recognized under the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples. Some Hanunuo leaders also represent their communities in consultations, ancestral land concerns, and development programs. Even with these modern political influences, traditional leadership and customary laws still remain important in guiding community life and preserving their cultural identity.

Political Reflection

The political life of the Hanunuo Mangyan is rooted in equality, cooperation, and respect, where there are no formal rulers, chiefs, or rigid hierarchies, and leadership is instead guided by respected elders and spiritual figures like the panudlakan who help maintain cultural and religious practices. Community decisions are generally made through open discussion and mutual agreement, and conflicts are resolved peacefully through dialogue rather than punishment, often with reconciliation as the main goal. Their political system is simple but effective, focusing on maintaining harmony within the community rather than exercising authority or control. Their administration and rules are based on customary laws that are not written but passed down through generations, shaping behavior, relationships, marriage, land use, and conflict resolution. Elders play a key role in interpreting these traditions and ensuring that peace is maintained, sometimes using practices like kasaba, a traditional trial by ordeal used for serious accusations, followed by fines or communal acts to restore balance. In modern times, they also interact with the Philippine government, barangay officials, and Indigenous organizations, which has introduced new forms of governance and representation. Despite these external influences, their customary laws and traditional leadership remain central in preserving their identity and guiding their way of life.

Economic

An economic landscape shaped by ancestral land, trade, craftsmanship, and the exchange of ideas.

Source of Living

The Hanunuo Mangyan people traditionally practice swidden (slash-and-burn) agriculture, cultivating upland rice, corn, sweet potatoes, taro, and other root crops as their main source of food. They also supplement their livelihood through foraging, hunting, and raising livestock, while some communities engage in small-scale trade by selling surplus crops and forest products such as bananas and ginger to lowlanders. In recent times, many farmers have started using commercial fertilizers and attending government-led agricultural seminars, where they learn modern farming techniques. Despite these changes, they continue to maintain their traditional planting practices alongside newer methods to improve harvests. In fishing communities, Hanunuo Mangyans who live near coastal areas traditionally used simple tools like spears and bows and arrows, but today many have shifted to using boats, including motorized ones, often influenced by neighboring Bisayan and Tagalog communities. Hunting, once more common in forested areas, has become less frequent due to the decline of wildlife and environmental protection laws such as the Wildlife Resources Conservation and Protection Act. Overall, while modernization and outside influences have introduced new tools and practices in farming, fishing, and hunting, the Hanunuo Mangyans continue to balance these changes with their traditional way of life.

Ancestral Land and Ancestral Domain

The ancestral land and ancestral domain of the Hanunuo Mangyan are deeply connected to their culture, livelihood, and identity as an indigenous people of Mindoro. The Hanunuo were originally coastal inhabitants but gradually moved to the mountainous interior because of colonization, invasions, and the arrival of lowland settlers. Their ancestral lands became the center of their daily life, where they practiced swidden farming (kaingin), planted rice and root crops, gathered forest resources, and built small communities composed of related families. These lands are not only sources of food and shelter but are also important in preserving their traditions, indigenous knowledge, and customs that have been passed down through generations. The ancestral domain of the Hanunuo extends beyond individual lands because it includes the forests, rivers, mountains, and natural resources that sustain their way of life and spiritual connection with nature. For the Hanunuo, their ancestral domain represents their collective history, identity, and right to self-determination as an indigenous community. However, the Hanunuo Mangyan have continuously faced threats to their ancestral land and domain due to logging concessions, mining operations, dams, biofuel plantations, and pasture lease agreements introduced by outsiders. During the 1970s, large areas in Mansalay, Bulalacao, and Roxas were converted into pasturelands owned by wealthy lowlanders, causing many Hanunuo families to lose access to territories they traditionally occupied and cultivated. These developments endangered not only their livelihood but also their culture and environment. Because of these issues, the Hanunuo organized themselves through indigenous groups to defend their ancestral domain and assert their indigenous rights. Protecting their ancestral lands and domains became important in preserving their traditions, political organization, environmental practices, and the future of their community.

Goods & Services

Among the Hanunuo Mangyan, trade in goods and services is mostly local and based on basic needs. They exchange surplus agricultural products such as upland rice, root crops, bananas, and ginger with lowland communities. In return, they receive essential goods like clothing, tools, and other household items that are not commonly produced within their mountainous settlements. This simple trading system helps them support their daily livelihood while maintaining their subsistence way of life. In terms of wider economic interaction, the Hanunuo Mangyan have limited participation in larger or international trade, but they are indirectly connected through lowland markets where their products are sold. Their economy remains largely traditional and community-based, focusing on self-sufficiency rather than large-scale commercial exchange. However, growing contact with nearby towns has gradually introduced them to broader market systems, influencing how they produce and sell their goods.

Technology & Ideas

Among the Hanunuo Mangyan, the transfer of technology and ideas happens mainly through interaction with elders, neighboring communities, and exposure to lowland Filipino society. Traditionally, knowledge such as farming methods, weaving, writing scripts, and cultural practices is passed down orally and through observation. Elders play a key role in teaching younger generations, ensuring that traditional skills and values are preserved. In recent times, new technologies and ideas have also been introduced through contact with government programs, education, and neighboring groups. These include modern farming techniques, tools, communication devices, and basic livelihood innovations. While these changes improve efficiency and access to resources, the Hanunuo Mangyan continue to balance modernization with the preservation of their traditional knowledge and cultural identity.

Economic Reflection

The economic life of the Hanunuo Mangyan is deeply rooted in their connection to ancestral land and traditional practices, where swidden agriculture remains their main source of livelihood. They grow upland rice, root crops, corn, and other plants for daily consumption, while also supplementing their needs through foraging, hunting, livestock raising, and small-scale trade with lowland communities. This way of life reflects their strong value on self-sufficiency and balance with nature, as they continue to rely on both traditional methods and gradually introduced modern farming techniques to sustain their needs. At the same time, their economic system extends beyond farming through simple trade and exchange of goods, where surplus produce is swapped for essential items like clothing, tools, and household materials that are not available in their mountainous areas. Although their participation in wider or international markets is limited, increasing contact with nearby towns has slowly influenced their economic activities. Alongside this, the transfer of knowledge and ideas—especially in farming, weaving, and cultural practices—remains strong, as elders continue to teach younger generations while also adapting to new technologies and external influences. This shows how their economy is both traditional and adaptive, balancing preservation with gradual change.

Cultural Reflection

Learning about the culture of the Hanunuo Mangyan gave me a deeper understanding and appreciation of the richness of indigenous traditions in the Philippines. Their culture reflects a simple yet meaningful way of life centered on respect for nature, family, community, and spirituality. I was amazed by how they preserve their identity through traditional practices such as weaving, bamboo carving, music, dance, poetry, and the use of the Surat Mangyan script. Their ambahan poetry, traditional musical instruments, rituals, and handwoven clothing show not only creativity and artistry but also the wisdom and values passed down through generations. The Hanunuo’s close relationship with nature also taught me the importance of caring for the environment and living in harmony with it. I also realized that despite the beauty and richness of their culture, the Hanunuo Mangyan continue to face many struggles such as discrimination, poverty, land conflicts, and the threat of losing their traditions due to modernization and outside influences. It is sad to know that many indigenous people are still misunderstood and treated unfairly because of their appearance, beliefs, and way of life. Their experiences made me reflect on how important it is to respect cultural diversity and protect the rights of indigenous communities. I admired how the Hanunuo continue to defend their ancestral land and preserve their customs even when faced with challenges and social discrimination. Their resilience and determination show how strongly they value their identity and heritage. Studying the Hanunuo Mangyan also made me realize that culture is not only about traditions or rituals but also about the identity, dignity, and history of a people. Their way of life teaches valuable lessons about unity, cooperation, humility, respect for elders, and appreciation for nature. Religion

Spiritual wisdom, ritual cadence, and sacred belief come together in atmospheric form.

✦

Ritual Practices

The tribe continues to practice its traditional rituals, including the “bugkos” ritual, where rice plants are tied to a stone, buried near the root area, and covered with soil while the community offers prayers together. This ritual, which has been passed down through generations, is believed to help increase the harvest yield. For the tribe, sustainable organic farming is mainly practiced to provide food for their families and community rather than for profit. Most of their produce is consumed within the community, but when there is surplus harvest, it is shared with other members of the tribe who are in need.

☾

Doctrines

Doctrines arise from cosmology, land-connected teachings, and values that guide daily lifeThe Hanunuo Mangyan do not have a written Bible or formal sacred text like other major religions because their beliefs and doctrines are preserved through oral traditions, rituals, stories, chants, and ambahan poetry passed down from generation to generation. Their spiritual teachings center on animism, or the belief that spirits inhabit nature such as trees, rivers, rocks, animals, and forests, and they emphasize the importance of living peacefully and harmoniously with the environment. They also believe in a Supreme Creator called Mahal na Makaako, who created and sustains the world. Their doctrines promote community cooperation, honesty, sharing resources, avoiding greed, respecting elders and ancestors, and preserving traditional rituals and practices, including ceremonies performed for protection, healing, and successful harvests.

⟡

Philosophical Beliefs

The Hanunuo Mangyan organize their lives around the principles of peace, harmony, and respectful interconnectedness with nature. Their philosophical and spiritual worldview is deeply influenced by animism, oral traditions such as ambahan poetry, and a strong reliance on subsistence living. They believe that the universe is inhabited by spirits, known as kalag, which exist in plants, rocks, forests, and other elements of nature, requiring humans to live in harmony with these forces. The Hanunuo also venerate a Supreme Creator called Mahal na Makaako, who is believed to have created all things and continues to watch over and sustain the world. Their social philosophy emphasizes respectful reciprocity, encouraging a way of life free from greed and excess while promoting cooperation, honesty, and hospitality within the community.

🌙

Gods & Goddesses

The Hanunuo Mangyan practice animism and believe in a complex spiritual system made up of nature spirits, ancestor spirits (kalag), and a Supreme Being, with their beliefs closely tied to daily agricultural life rather than a large pantheon. At the center of their spirituality is Mahal na Makaako, the Supreme Being and creator who is believed to have given life to humans simply by looking at them. They also honor Binayi, the sacred guardian of the garden where spirits rest, who is strongly associated with agriculture and rice production, where upsetting her is believed to cause poor harvests. Another important figure is Binayo, a female spirit who cares for the rice spirits (kalag paray) and must be respected and appeased through rituals during planting and harvest seasons. In their mythology, Bulungabon is a powerful spirit accompanied by twelve fierce dogs that chase erring or sinful souls, reflecting their belief in spiritual consequences for human actions.

✶

Religious Behavior

The Hanunuo Mangyan believe that everything in nature—plants, animals, rocks, and even objects—has a spirit, and this shapes the way they live every day, from farming and health to their ideas about right and wrong. At the center of their belief is Mahal na Makaako, the Supreme Being who is said to have created humans just by looking at them. They also believe in kalag, or guardian spirits that look after nature and people, which is why they follow certain rules, avoid breaking taboos, and give offerings like food or beads to show respect. On the other hand, they also believe in labang, harmful spirits that can bring sickness or bad luck if the natural and spiritual balance is disturbed. Because of this, they try to live carefully and respectfully with nature, believing that harmony with the spirit world keeps their lives safe and peaceful.

⚡

Consequential Beliefs

The consequential beliefs of the Hanunuo Mangyan are the beliefs and spiritual practices that greatly influence their daily life, decisions, traditions, and relationship with the environment. The Hanunuo believe in a Supreme Being as well as different spirits that inhabit nature, forests, rivers, and surrounding places. They believe that these spiritual beings can affect human life by bringing protection, guidance, blessings, sickness, or misfortune depending on how people behave toward nature and the spirit world. Because of this belief, the Hanunuo perform rituals, prayers, and offerings during important events such as planting, harvesting, healing illnesses, and community gatherings. Rituals are often led by respected elders or spiritual healers who are believed to have knowledge and connection with the spiritual world. Through these practices, the Hanunuo seek balance, safety, and harmony between humans, nature, and spirits. Their consequential beliefs also influence their values, social behavior, and environmental practices. The Hanunuo strongly believe that nature must be respected because forests, mountains, and rivers are sacred and are connected to spiritual beings and their ancestors. This belief encourages them to care for the environment and avoid actions that may destroy natural resources. They also value unity, cooperation, respect for elders, and obedience to customary traditions because these help maintain peace and order within the community. During times of sickness, disasters, or hardships, the Hanunuo rely not only on practical solutions but also on rituals and indigenous knowledge for protection and healing. Their beliefs strengthen their resilience and cultural identity because they continue to preserve traditional practices passed down from generation to generation despite modernization and outside influences.

Religious Reflection

The spiritual life of the Hanunuo Mangyan is deeply rooted in a worldview that connects nature, community, and the spirit world as one continuous system of meaning. Their rituals, such as the “bugkos,” reflect how everyday activities like farming are not only practical but also sacred, as they are performed with prayers and collective participation to ensure harmony and good harvests. Instead of relying on written scriptures, their doctrines are preserved through oral traditions, ambahan poetry, and cultural practices that emphasize respect for nature, cooperation, and balance in life. Their beliefs also show a strong philosophical foundation centered on animism and the presence of spirits (kalag) in all elements of nature, along with faith in a Supreme Creator, Mahal na Makaako, who is believed to sustain life. These beliefs guide their behavior, encouraging respectful living, avoidance of greed, and maintaining harmony with both the environment and the spiritual world. Their mythology, which includes figures like Binayi, Binayo, and Bulungabon, reflects how they understand moral consequences through spiritual balance rather than formal punishment systems. Overall, their religion is not just a set of beliefs but a way of life that shapes how they think, act, and relate to the world around them.

Issues

Education, health and Poverty & Livelihood are vital priorities for Indigenous communities today.

Community Challenges

These are the most pressing issues facing the Hanunuo Mangyan today, shaping their resilience and future well-being.

Education

The Hanunuo Mangyan are one of the indigenous groups living in the mountainous areas of Mindoro. Despite their rich culture and traditions, many Hanunuo Mangyan children face difficulties in accessing quality education. Schools are often far from their communities, making transportation challenging and expensive. Poverty also limits their ability to buy school supplies and continue studying. In addition, some students experience discrimination and language barriers because lessons are usually taught in Filipino or English instead of their native language. To address these challenges, programs promoting culture-based education and Mother Tongue-Based Multilingual Education have been introduced to help preserve Hanunuo Mangyan identity while improving learning opportunities. Some schools and organizations also teach Surat Mangyan and traditional ambahan poetry to keep their culture alive. Education plays an important role in empowering the Hanunuo Mangyan community, helping younger generations gain knowledge and opportunities while still valuing and preserving their indigenous heritage.

Health

Previous Mangyan studies have shown that they have poor health, are generally malnourished and continually susceptible to communicable diseases. The need to develop viable strategies to counter their health problems is pressing since Mindoro suffers from insufficient health care facilities and personnel, and health services are rarely available to Mangyans. Baseline surveys on the health and nutritional status of the Hanunuo population in the project site indicated that the following illnesses are most prevalent: upper respiratory tract infection, skin diseases, parasitism, anaemia and malaria. The more significant health problems of the Hanunuo include poor environmental sanitation, lack of medical personnel and drugs, inadequate knowledge of curative and preventive care, lack of adequate prenatal care, poor nutritional status and lack of health education. Also described are the participation of the community in project planning and decision making, the training workshops and resource manual for the community volunteers, the coverage areas and assigned tasks of the health workers, and the organisation of village health committees.

Poverty & Livelihood

The community mainly depends on farming and forest resources for their daily living. Many families practice kaingin or swidden farming, where they plant rice, corn, bananas, root crops, and other vegetables for food and income. However, poverty remains a major challenge because of limited access to education, healthcare, transportation, and stable livelihood opportunities. Climate change, droughts, and typhoons also affect their crops, making it difficult for families to earn enough to support their daily needs. Despite these hardships, the Hanunuo Mangyan continue to preserve their traditional way of life and deep connection with nature. Women in the community also contribute by making handicrafts and doing household work while helping in farming activities. Their sustainable farming practices show their respect for the environment, contrary to misconceptions that they destroy forests through kaingin. With proper government support, livelihood programs, and recognition of indigenous rights, the Hanunuo Mangyan community can improve their quality of life while preserving their cultural heritage.

Land and Displacement

The Hanunuo Mangyan continue to face serious issues concerning their ancestral land and forests in Mindoro. One of the biggest problems is the threat of mining operations and commercial exploitation within their ancestral domain. Large areas of forest land claimed by the Mangyan tribes are believed to contain valuable minerals such as gold and natural gas, attracting mining companies and outside investors. Because of this, many Hanunuo and other Mangyan communities fear displacement from the lands they have occupied and protected for generations. Tribal leaders expressed concern that if mining companies are allowed to operate in these areas, their land, forests, culture, and heritage could be permanently destroyed. Another major issue is the difficulty of securing legal ownership of their ancestral lands. Although indigenous communities have rights under Philippine law, the Hanunuo Mangyan often struggle to provide formal documents or land titles because many communities rely on oral history and traditional boundaries instead of written records. Their isolation, poverty, and limited access to education make it harder for them to process legal requirements and defend their land claims. As a result, overlapping claims from mining companies, settlers, and government projects continue to threaten their ancestral domain. The Hanunuo also face environmental and livelihood problems related to land use. Their traditional swidden farming or "kaingin" has often been criticized and blamed for deforestation, even though the Hanunuo believe their farming practices are sustainable and respectful of nature. They practice controlled farming with fallow periods that allow forests to recover and avoid cutting sacred forests or large trees. However, misunderstandings about indigenous farming methods continue to create conflict between the Hanunuo and government environmental policies. In addition, droughts, land encroachment, and poverty have affected their crops and food security, making life more difficult for many Hanunuo families.

Discrimination

The Hanunuo Mangyan have long experienced discrimination and marginalization, especially in education and interactions with lowland society. As lowland Filipinos migrated to Mindoro for land and economic opportunities, the Hanunuo gradually became minorities within their own ancestral territory. Many Hanunuo communities moved deeper into the mountainous interior to maintain their autonomy and protect their culture. However, because of poverty, isolation, and limited access to formal education, they were often viewed negatively by outsiders and treated as “uncivilized” or inferior. Their traditional clothing, long hair, language, and indigenous customs became reasons for prejudice and social exclusion. Discrimination became especially evident in formal schools. Hanunuo children attending lowland schools were frequently harassed, mocked, and belittled by classmates and even teachers. Boys were discouraged from wearing their traditional loincloths and pressured to cut their long hair to conform to lowland standards. Because education was heavily based on lowland Filipino culture, many Hanunuo felt alienated and feared losing their indigenous identity. Some community members used the term “Pinoy Mangyan” to describe individuals who became too influenced by lowland culture and were no longer seen as “real” Mangyan by their own community. This created conflict between the desire for education and the need to preserve their traditions and cultural identity. The Hanunuo also experienced discrimination when traveling outside their communities. Tribal leaders who visited cities for meetings related to the Pundasyon Hanunoo Mangyan School project were subjected to verbal abuse because of their appearance and traditional clothing. Due to this treatment, some leaders eventually wore trousers and shirts while traveling to avoid harassment and humiliation. These experiences highlighted the challenges faced by indigenous peoples in gaining respect and equal treatment within wider Philippine society. In response, the Hanunuo established culturally sensitive educational programs such as the Pundasyon Hanunoo Mangyan School to help preserve their culture while giving children the skills needed to defend their rights and participate in modern society. Issues Reflection

Learning about the issues faced by the Hanunuo Mangyan made me realize how difficult life can be for indigenous communities who continue to preserve their culture despite poverty, limited education, and lack of opportunities. Many Hanunuo Mangyan families live in remote areas where access to schools, healthcare, and transportation is limited. Because of these challenges, many children are unable to continue their studies, while families struggle to earn enough income for their daily needs. Education programs for the Hanunuo Mangyan help provide learning opportunities while still respecting and preserving their indigenous culture and traditions. I also realized that the Hanunuo Mangyan are often misunderstood by society. Some people look down on their traditional way of living without understanding the importance of their culture and practices. Aside from discrimination, they also face problems involving their ancestral land and environment. Indigenous communities in the Philippines, including Mangyan groups, continue to struggle to protect their forests and ancestral lands from outside threats. Despite these challenges, the Hanunuo Mangyan continue to protect their traditions, language, and environment. Their strong connection to nature and their simple way of life show discipline, resilience, and respect for their cultural identity. This made me appreciate the value of indigenous knowledge and traditions even more. This topic taught me the importance of respecting and supporting indigenous communities instead of ignoring their struggles. The Hanunuo Mangyan are an important part of the cultural diversity of the Philippines, and they deserve equal opportunities and recognition. As students and members of society, we should help spread awareness about their situation and support programs that promote education, healthcare, livelihood, and the preservation of indigenous culture for future generations.
